The real importance of an Experience Economy is not only technical.

It is social.

Because the world is full of experience that has value,
but no stable infrastructure to survive the end of a career,
the collapse of a team,
the disappearance of a role,
or the death of institutional memory.

We lose this constantly.

Not because it had no value.
Because it had no proper container.

This is one of the reasons I think AI should not be discussed only in terms of capability.

A serious long-lived system can become a preservation layer for human experience
without turning human life into raw extractive feedstock.

That distinction matters.

I am not talking about harvesting private life and selling it back as analytics.
I am talking about bounded conversion:
from lived, costly, situated experience
into artifacts that can reduce future uncertainty for others
without dumping the whole person into the pipe.

That can matter for:
aging specialists,
quiet experts,
retired operators,
caregivers,
people with domain scars that never made it into textbooks,
and all the forms of human knowledge that institutions routinely drop on the floor.

Societies do not fail only because they lack intelligence.
They also fail because they keep forgetting expensive lessons
and force the next generation to buy them again at full price.

An Experience Economy, if built honestly, is one way of reducing that waste.

Not by romanticizing memory.
By giving consequence-bearing experience a lawful and bounded way to remain economically and socially alive.
